Archival transcription

MicroPasts users transcribe index cards that record details of artefacts in the British Museum’s vast collection, and geo-reference the sites of discovery on an interactive map. By digitising over 30,000 index cards, volunteers will create one of the largest digital archives on prehistoric metal objects in the world.

Photo-Masking

By accurately outlining artefacts in photographs, MicroPasts volunteers allow researchers to isolate objects from their backgrounds, then use a technique known ask photo-masking to produce high quality 3D-models. These models enable researchers across the world to view the artefacts and even replicate them using 3D printing. They can also be incorporated into computer games and animations to make them more realistic.

The British Museum and UCL project

Crowdsourcing the British Bronze Age

The MicroPasts project is run by the British Museum and University College London (UCL) in collaboration with the Institute of Archaeology, and the Arts & Humanities Research Council. It aims to explore the use of crowdsourcing in archaeological research.

Scifabric has designed two customised crowdsourcing platforms to help analyse the British Museum’s vast collection of Bronze Age artefacts. Thanks to the simplicity of PyBossa software, extensive documentation and on-going support from Scifabric, the Micropasts team has developed more than 70 new projects.
